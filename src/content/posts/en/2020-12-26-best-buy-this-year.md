Seeing how many people were writing "Best Buys" articles as year-end blog content, I decided I'd give it a shot too.

I thought about what I've bought and decided to introduce the first things that came to mind.

## Speakers

I think everyone has a different idea of what a speaker is, but the ones I bought are the YAMAHA [HS5](https://www.soundhouse.co.jp/products/detail/item/184221/) studio monitor speakers. I believe I bought them around April while I was working from home.

These speakers are frequently used in music production (DTM) and small broadcast studios. They are a standard choice for those purposes, and since I had never had any complaints using them at work, I happily welcomed them into my home.

As they are powered speakers, they don't require an external amplifier; you can just connect the output from your audio interface directly to the speaker inputs, and you're good to go. If you already have an audio interface, these speakers are all you need.

They sound incredibly good for how easy they are to set up. Of course, even if you set them up haphazardly, the sound imaging will likely be off, so I’ve been precise about their placement.

Here is what I am careful about when setting them up:

- Position them so that they form an approximate equilateral triangle with my sitting position.
- Set them at a height where the tweeters are roughly level with my ears.
- Place them so there are no obstructions like monitors in front of the speaker diaphragms.
- Avoid placing them too close to the wall.
    - At least 30cm away.
- Keep the bass at a moderate setting so as not to disturb the neighbors.
    - Also, keep the volume within a reasonable range.
    - If you don't live in a reinforced concrete building, using speakers might be challenging to begin with.

There is a switch on the speakers that allows you to adjust the bass in three steps, which I make use of.

If you find that setting insufficient, it's best to buy an audio interface that allows for equalization on the output. Depending on the room layout or placement, some sound bands might sound overly prominent, and for these non-bass frequencies, using an equalizer is really the only way to address it.

To check which frequency bands are too strong, you can use a tone generator website like [this one](https://www.szynalski.com/tone-generator/) to isolate and play specific frequency ranges. In my room, there was a band between 100-200Hz that sounded too sharp, so I used the equalizer to notch it down just enough so it no longer bothers me.

I’m not sure if this is the "correct" method, but it made the sound image feel much clearer to me, so I’m satisfied.

Also, I use a 32-inch monitor, so I have them placed on both sides of it. If you are using dual 24-27 inch monitors and want them pushed right up against each other, you probably wouldn't have the space to place these speakers.

The sound quality is completely different from the desktop USB speakers you can get for a few thousand yen. While earphones and headphones can be physically bothersome to wear for long periods, being freed from that with speakers makes for a much better experience.

It would be interesting to incorporate them into a home theater system (though I’ve never bought a soundbar, so I can't compare).

By the way, regarding speakers, based on my experience when I was in the audio technology research club in college, I have a (somewhat extreme) theory that a larger diaphragm diameter equals better sound, a larger cabinet volume equals better sound, and higher peak volume capacity equals better sound.

## iPhone SE (2nd Generation)

I talked way too much about speakers, so next is the smartphone.

Until I switched to the SE, I was using the iPhone 11 Pro Max.

Because I’m wearing a mask all the time, I couldn't use Face ID, which was a huge source of stress. Since it was announced that the iPhone 12 series wouldn't feature Touch ID, I bought the SE immediately after the Apple event. I think it was around October.

As I mentioned, I was originally using the largest model, so it was physically much smaller, but I got used to it quickly.

Battery life is quite poor; just listening to music during my commute and work hours puts it at under 20% by the time I head home. However, lately, I’ve felt that staring at my smartphone doesn't add much productivity, or rather, it consumes time without providing much in return, so the battery life isn't a huge complaint for me.

My biggest dissatisfaction is with the camera. I sometimes record myself playing IIDX at game centers using the front-facing camera. The 11 Pro Max could record at presumably 60fps, but the SE is capped at 30fps.

Also, since video recording consumes a lot of battery, I have to be careful when shooting on my way home from work. Even so, since recording isn't essential, it's not a huge deal anyway.

For next year, I hope for a solution that allows for comfortable unlocking while wearing a mask, such as the return of Touch ID.
