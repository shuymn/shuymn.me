Setting aside the fact that the title sounds a bit grandiose, I was working on a hobby project at my parents' house using machine learning (supervised learning), and I found that while I could relatively easily come up with programs to solve toy problems and develop them at a decent speed, it felt quite difficult to conceive or build more practical (realistic) software.

I’d think, "This is great!" when an idea first strikes, but as I’d progress with the development, I’d start realizing, "Actually, this is a bit inconvenient in this case," or "Does this even have any real use case to begin with?"

And to overcome that, things end up becoming a massive undertaking, leaving me unsure if it's even feasible, and the project eventually falls into a labyrinth.

Looking at sites like Qiita, there are plenty of things where it's clear the technical task can be done, but when I consider whether there's an actual real-world problem being solved by it, I often wonder, "Wait, isn't there not?"

On deeper reflection, it feels like this is just part of the difficulty of product development in general, and perhaps not limited to machine learning. But since I’ve already written this much, I’ll post it as is.

I feel like if I worry too much about only posting perfect or highly reliable information, I’ll reach a point where I can’t write any articles at all.

For now, I intend to finish the program I'm working on and make it open source. Even if I can't think of a use case for it myself, by making it public, someone else might find one.
