Since information slips out of my mind quickly if I just read it, I decided to write a book review.

## The Book I Read

[Introduction to Anger Management](https://www.amazon.co.jp/dp/B01MZE5NCK)

As the name suggests, anger management explains specific techniques for controlling yourself when you feel "anger" so that it does not negatively impact your mental state or those around you. This book focuses primarily on "anger" in business settings.

### Chapter 1

The first chapter covers themes such as "Why are people controlled by anger?" I felt there was an Adlerian psychological perspective in the segment stating, "You choose to be angry yourself."

Also, regarding the common observation that "modern people have become more temperamental than in the past," there were observations based on recent social conditions that I felt were quite persuasive.

### Chapter 2

The second chapter included a section often found in books that combine psychology and business, discussing "anger as an animal"—the idea that "instinctively, it is like this (therefore, here is my argument)." I feel that the "animal instincts" mentioned in such sections are often of dubious validity and frequently rely on the author's subjectivity or human misconceptions, so I took that part with a grain of salt.

There was also a discussion about the steps involved in how humans perceive anger, specifically: "perception of events → interpretation of events → generation of anger." It explained that the second step, "interpretation of events," is crucial and detailed how to handle it.

Furthermore, there is a concept called a "core belief," which, as far as I can interpret, is almost synonymous with "values." Since the "interpretation of events" is based on core beliefs, the book stresses the importance of understanding one's own core beliefs.

Then, there was an explanation of the overall picture of anger management, stating that the specific tasks are "correcting behavior" and "correcting perceptions."

"Correcting behavior" refers to "not acting while angry," while "correcting perceptions" means "fixing the core beliefs that generate anger."

### Chapter 3 and Beyond

From this point on, the focus was on specific approaches, so my takeaway was to keep the ones that seem practical in the back of my mind.

Regarding "correcting perceptions," it mentions correcting the core beliefs—the values within oneself that trigger anger. While it was written quite simply, I felt that in reality, it is actually quite difficult to change one's own values just because they caused frustration. My take is that it is precisely because one's values are not flexible that anger occurs, and perhaps values that can be easily modified are not the ones that generate anger in the first place.

## Conclusion

Whether big or small, I encounter situations at work where I feel anger, and since I feel that my ability to control anger is immature, I want to keep the approaches introduced in this book in mind and put them into practice.
