## Background

To discuss this topic, I first need to establish a shared understanding of my English reading proficiency.

To put it simply, my skills in reading, writing, and listening to English are insufficient.

For context, the last time I formally studied English was for university entrance exams, and my ability has remained largely stagnant since then.

### National Center Test

- Written exam: Around 150 points (out of 200, so about 75%)
- Listening: Around 40 points (out of 50, so about 80%)

This was about five years ago, so my memory is a bit hazy.

### TOEIC

I recall scoring around 360 points.

I think I took this test when I entered university around 2018.

I can almost hear people thinking, "How can you live with such an embarrassing score?" but that is the reality, and I have to get on with my life regardless, so please forgive me.

## The Main Point

First, when I say "technical information," I mean blog posts about Software Engineering. You can think of it as the equivalent of articles on Qiita or Zenn.

In short, **I translate them using DeepL and read them, and I intentionally avoid taking the time to read them as if I were studying English.**

Here, I will explain "why I translate" and "why I don't try to study English at the same time."

### Why I translate

It is common to end up with English results when researching technical topics. This presents the choice of either "avoiding" them or finding a way to "read" them. While it would be ideal to understand everything through Japanese sources alone, some documentation is only written in English, making it difficult to get by without reading any English at all.

When trying to read a text in a non-native language, I think there are two options: "translate it into your native language and read it" or "read it as is." One might also consider "asking someone else," but I don't count on relying on others.

As for me, I find it difficult to "read as is." Even if I couldn't read at the same speed as Japanese, if I could comprehend English at 60-70% of that speed, perhaps I could read it as is (but I cannot do that).

Therefore, I translate it into Japanese.

### Why I don't treat it as English study

I think it would be fine to combine them if the objective for "gathering technical information" and the objective for "studying English" were the same, but in most cases, they are not.

If I were to look up the meaning of every piece of grammar, word, or idiom, I would have nowhere near the speed required to achieve the goal that lies beyond "gathering technical information." Furthermore, looking things up doesn't mean I will understand them immediately. For an inexperienced person like me, information gathering is about quantity over quality.

If I tried to force myself to study, I would end up in a negative spiral where the very act of gathering information in English becomes a burden. I think it would be counterproductive if, despite having the ambition to expand my research scope to include English, that very act ended up making me hate English. Because I started to fall into that state, I decided to set aside my goal of becoming proficient in English and rely on translation instead.

## Conclusion

If you chase two rabbits, you will catch neither.
