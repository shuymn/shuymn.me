Despite being a working professional, I had been in a state where my parents were paying for my mobile phone bills. Since I had some free time, I decided it was a good opportunity to start paying for my own expenses. I also took the time to review my plans. Since I didn't get too granular with it, please forgive me if some plan names or prices are slightly off.

The devices I use are as follows:

- iPhone
    - Primary phone number I've used for a long time, includes voice calls.
    - Always carried when going out.
- Apple Watch
    - Always carried whenever I go out (except for things like taking out the trash).
    - Cellular model.
- iPad
    - Mainly used at home. I bring it to work or on trips, but rarely use it.
- Android Smartphone
    - I have it just in case, but rarely use it. The battery is often dead.
    - Used for redundancy of my fixed home internet.
    - This became relevant after I overhauled my home network at the end of last year and introduced an RTX1210.

### iPhone

Previously, I wasn't paying anything. It was a shared plan with my family for data capacity. Looking at the history, I seem to use about 3GB per month. The options were:

- Stay with docomo
    - 5G Giga Light: 5,000–6,000 yen/month
    - ahamo: 3,000 yen
- MNP to another provider
    - povo2.0 (au): Base 0 yen/month
    - Apparently, they cancel your line if you don't purchase paid add-ons for a certain period.
    - There are various others, but I haven't investigated them.

Since I don't go out very often, I didn't think I would use up the 20GB of data on ahamo, so I went with the pay-as-you-go style of povo2.0.

Since my device is a dual-SIM model, I decided to use an IIJmio eSIM for data communication. That's 440 yen per month for 2GB.

The reason I chose IIJmio is that if you contract multiple plans with the same company, they offer a data sharing feature that allows you to aggregate and divide all that data.

### Apple Watch

With docomo, there is a service called the "One Number Service" that allows the Apple Watch to communicate and make calls using the iPhone's mobile line, but this service does not exist for ahamo or povo2.0.

It is frustrating that my cellular model is effectively becoming a Wi-Fi-only model, but I gave up on it because I didn't want to spend nearly 10,000 yen per month on mobile lines.

There was an incident in the past where I went out without my phone, only wearing my Apple Watch, and got locked out of my apartment by the auto-lock system. Thanks to the cellular model, I was able to use data to look up public transportation and go to the real estate agency I used to move in, but if that happens again, I’ll just have to walk there...

### iPad

I had a DMM mobile data & SMS SIM, a 1GB per month plan. It cost about 700 yen a month.

I switched this to an IIJmio Giga Plan eSIM with 2GB per month. That costs 440 yen per month.

By the way, looking at my DMM mobile history, I hardly used any cellular data at all. When you have lines you effectively don't use, the data sharing feature found in services like IIJmio is very welcome.

### Android Smartphone

I had a Pixel 3a, but recently bought an Xperia 5 III (docomo version). There isn't much to do on it other than using Twitter on the 120Hz display. The camera is high-spec, so I'm thinking of carrying it with me whenever I go out.

I was using Rakuten Mobile for the line, which was 0 yen per month since I rarely used it either. I plan to change this to an IIJmio physical SIM with voice calling. It hasn't arrived yet, and because I was doing some trial and error, I’m currently on a data-only contract. I'll need to change the contract (and pay a high fee) once it arrives, so this setup will stay as is for a while.

IIJmio's price difference between a data SIM and a voice SIM is only about 100 yen, so I figured I might as well have a phone number for the device. I originally intended to use Rakuten Mobile, but as mentioned above, the frequency of me carrying it might increase. Rakuten Mobile is 0 yen up to 1GB, but it costs 1,000 yen for up to 3GB, which I was concerned about. By using IIJmio, it adds to my shared data pool, so I can use it without worrying about that.

The domestic carrier version of the Xperia 5 III doesn't support dual-SIM, so I have no choice but to use a physical SIM. Otherwise, I might have just kept using Rakuten + IIJ.

### Fixed Line Redundancy

I was previously using a NURO Mobile trial plan (SoftBank line). It was about 300 yen per month.

I was thinking of using an IIJmio data SIM, but after much deliberation, I decided to put a Rakuten Mobile SIM in it. Docomo MVNO lines like IIJmio can sometimes drop below 1Mbps in download speed during peak hours, which is unreliable. However, since my home is in the Rakuten coverage area and this is specifically for redundancy, the drawback of Rakuten Mobile being hard to connect to in some regions can be completely ignored. Additionally, since data is effectively unlimited, I expect that even if my apartment's equipment fails and I can't use my main line for a few days, I won't have to worry about data limits. It is a line with voice calls, which is a slight downside, but with Rakuten Mobile, having a voice line doesn't affect the price, so I decided to ignore it as it's just a matter of principle.

## Summary

Various service fees are "priceless" (I say while looking away). Specifically, IIJmio charges 3,000 yen per line, but they happen to have a campaign going on now that cuts that in half, so I feel like I got away with paying about 5,000 yen total.

Also, after signing up for IIJmio, I discovered an MVNO called LinksMate (run by a CyberAgent subsidiary). They have data sharing and it feels like it would have been about 200 yen/month cheaper in total. However, I convinced myself that IIJmio is a well-established company, so it must be safer. If the number of data-only lines I need increases, I might consider switching to LinksMate. I don't plan on adding any more lines, so I hope it doesn't come to that...

Personally, I wanted to keep my mobile costs around 2,000 yen per month, so I am satisfied.
